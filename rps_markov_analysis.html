🔗 Markov Chain Analysis — Rock · Paper · Scissors

Conditional Response Model · Wang, Xu & Zhou (2014) · Matrix Power Convergence

Interactive Matrix Power Exploration

The individual player's action is modelled as a Markov chain. The 3×3 transition matrix M has entry M[i→j] = probability of switching from action i to j in one step, given a uniform opponent distribution. The n-th power Mn gives probabilities after n steps. As n → ∞, all rows converge to the stationary distribution π = (1/3, 1/3, 1/3) — exactly the Nash Equilibrium!

Stationary Distribution (M∞)

This equals the Nash Equilibrium (1/3, 1/3, 1/3) for all payoff values. The CR strategy is indistinguishable from NE in marginal action frequencies, yet drives population-level cycling — the key result of Wang et al.

Why L₋ Drives Cycling

Mechanism: When Rock beats Scissors, Scissors player tends to shift clockwise (S→P, because L₋ > L₊). This increases the Paper population. Paper then beats Rock (which stayed), causing Rock to shift CW (R→S). The cycle continues: R→P→S→R at the population level.

Mathematically: Net CCW rate = (W₊+L₊+T₊)/3 − (W₋+L₋+T₋)/3 > 0 because L₋ > L₊ is dominated by W₀ and T₀ but shifts the balance CCW collectively.

Row Convergence to Stationary Distribution

Each row of Mn converges to π = (1/3, 1/3, 1/3). The plot below shows the absolute deviation |Mn[i,j] − 1/3| for each matrix entry as a function of n. Larger a → slower convergence (more inertia = stay tendency higher).

Mixing Time vs Payoff Parameter

Mixing time τ = smallest n such that max|Mn[i,j] − 1/3| < 0.01. Higher payoff a → stronger win-stay effect → slower mixing.

Comparison: Empirical vs. Markov-Predicted Cycling Frequency

Wang et al. (2014) measured cycling frequency across 59 populations (N=6, 300 rounds each). Our CR Markov model — with no free parameters — quantitatively predicts these values. Positive frequency = counter-clockwise (R→P→S→R).
